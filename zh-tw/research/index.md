### 精選工作論文


- Knocking on the Wrong Door: Partisan Canvassing and Asymmetric Belief Updating in Britain.  (with <u><a href="https://sites.google.com/view/litang2020" style="color: #9ab8b8;">Li Tang</a></u>)
  <details class="abstract" style="font-weight: bold;">
    <summary>Abstract <br> (Conference: 2026 東吳國會研討會 ⬥ 2026 EPSS ⬥ 2026 APSA )</summary>
    <div style="font-size: 0.9em; font-weight: normal;">    
    Door-to-door canvassing shapes how voters perceive parties' positions, yet its effects may be asymmetric depending on partisan alignment. To test this, we develop a model of identity-weighted belief updating in which partisan identity leads voters to weight ideologically congruent signals from co-partisan canvassers more heavily and discount messages from rival parties. Drawing on nine waves of the British Election Study (2014–2024; N ≈ 12,700), we find empirical support for this model: own-party contact substantially pulls voter perceptions closer to the perceived party position, whereas rival-party contact does not. Moreover, voters contacted by their own party report wider ideological distance between the major parties. These findings thus point to a troubling possibility: campaign mobilisation may reinforce ideological sorting as an unintended effect.
    </div>  
  </details>



- Cross-Lingual Stance Detection in Political Texts: Comparison and Application. (with <u><a href="https://muellerstefan.net" style="color: #9ab8b8;">Stefan Müller</a></u>)
  <details class="abstract" style="font-weight: bold;">
    <summary>Abstract <br> (Conference: 2026 PolMeth Europe)</summary>
    <div style="font-size: 0.9em; font-weight: normal;">    
     In this paper, we benchmark automated stance annotation frameworks using the European Parliament coal debate corpus from Benoit et al. (2016)—36 MEP speeches in six languages, crowd-coded separately for each language alongside corresponding vote decisions. We compare fine-tuned multilingual transformer models (fine-tuned on the English-only PoliStance (Burnham, 2024) and multilingual X-Stance (Vamvas and Sennrich, 2020) datasets, the latter covering German, Italian, and English) and small, lightweight open-source decoder LLMs — Qwen 2.5 (7B), Gemma 3 (4B), and Mistral (3B) — prompted under zero-shot, fixed few-shot, and retrieval-augmented (RAG) strategies without any fine-tuning. Our results show that LLMs substantially outperform fine-tuned transformers across all languages and metrics, with retrieval-augmented Qwen 2.5 performing best overall, and that annotation errors are higher where even human coders disagree. Our findings suggest that small open-source LLMs with retrieval augmentation offer a more valid alternative to human coders for cross-lingual political text annotation, albeit at greater computational cost, though this is manageable with parallelisation on HPC clusters or high-performance workstations.
    </div>  
  </details>

- AI Survey Respondents as Measurement Instruments: A Pre-Registered Total Survey Error Framework. (with <u><a href="https://linettelim.com" style="color: #9ab8b8;">Linette Lim</a></u> and  <u><a href="https://sjankin.com" style="color: #9ab8b8;">Slava Jankin</a></u>)  _Under Review_
  <details class="abstract" style="font-weight: bold;">
    <summary>Abstract <br> </summary>
    <div style="font-size: 0.9em; font-weight: normal;">    
    Survey researchers routinely characterise the error properties of their instruments before drawing inferences. AI respondents have largely escaped this discipline. They are deployed with increasing frequency, but rarely benchmarked against matched human data using pre-specified criteria. The result is a validation gap: researchers cannot distinguish what these instruments recover reliably from what they distort.
    This paper applies the Total Survey Error tradition to AI respondents. Four alignment metrics, mapped onto established TSE concepts, are specified with fixed tolerance thresholds before any AI output is examined. The design pairs 1,135 UK adults in a pre-registered misinformation correction experiment with individually matched AI agent twins, each constructed from the actual pre-treatment survey responses of their human counterpart and exposed to identical stimuli under the same conditions.
    The results define an operating envelope. AI respondents recover the direction of correction effects for all claims and both outcome types. Subgroup sign patterns hold across ideology terciles. For the highest-salience claim, magnitude alignment is close to exact. Beyond that boundary, the instrument fails in identifiable ways: it over-reduces sharing intentions by roughly two to three times, produces a spurious ideology gradient absent in human data, and shows no individual-level correspondence between matched pairs. A calibration offset of approximately 0.4 scale points is consistent across claims, predictable in sign, and analogous to a conventional mode effect. The human arm of the study also reveals thematic spillover across same-domain claims, a finding with broader implications for sequential survey design. Cross-model replication confirms that directional properties generalise; calibration and artefact profiles do not.
    The benchmarked instrument is applied to Taiwan cross-strait misinformation during the 2024 presidential election, where running a human experiment is ethically constrained. Correction effects vary with political predisposition in the directions theory would expect, bounded by the operating envelope mapped in the UK study. The framework provides what the field currently lacks: a replicable disclosure standard that makes AI respondent error measurable.
</div>
</details>

- Multi-Agent LLM Systems for Synthetic Survey Experiments in Ethically Constrained Settings. (with <u><a href="https://linettemlim.github.io" style="color: #9ab8b8;">Linette Lim</a></u> and <u><a href="https://sjankin.com" style="color: #9ab8b8;">Slava Jankin</a></u>)   {{< badge href="https://www.dropbox.com/scl/fi/pnpv3t1dqji7qxnuoihqx/Multi_Agent_Systems_with_LLMs.pdf?rlkey=du7hl5mainlk6lp0a0ki6b5f0&dl=0" icon="fa-file-pdf" text="PDF" >}}
  <details class="abstract" style="font-weight: bold;">
    <summary>Abstract <br> (Conference: 2025 PolMeth Europe ⬥ 2025 CwC-LLM Workshop EPSA ⬥ 2025 APSA)</summary>
    <div style="font-size: 0.9em; font-weight: normal;">    
    Experimental studies of misinformation often face ethical constraints because they expose human participants to false or harmful content. We propose an LLM-based multi-agent framework, implemented in AG2, that reproduces core features of survey experiments in a fully synthetic environment, avoiding direct exposure of human subjects to misinformation. We calibrate a population of 1,140 synthetic agents to rich Taiwanese voter survey data and examine, within this population, susceptibility to misinformation and the effectiveness of fact-checking interventions under randomized assignment. Agents with more pro-China predispositions exhibit higher acceptance of misinformation. Corrective information substantially reduces credibility ratings -- from 2.5 to 1.0 on a five-point scale in the treatment group relative to controls -- yet correlations between political attitudes and misinformation susceptibility persist after controlling for demographics. We make the multi-agent design and implementation fully transparent, releasing code and agent specifications, and argue that such synthetic survey experiments can complement, rather than replace, human-subject studies by enabling pre-testing of experimental designs and exploration of ethically sensitive scenarios.

    </div>    
  </details>
  
- Game-Theoretic Multi-Agent Systems with LLMs for Crisis Negotiation and Simulation. (with <u><a href="" style="color: #9ab8b8; text-decoration: underline;">Shuli Zhang</a></u>)<br>
  <details class="abstract" style="font-weight: bold;">
    <summary>Abstract <br> (Conference: 2026 CwC-LLM Workshop SPSA ⬥ 11th Behavioral Models of Politics Conference)</summary>
    <div style="font-size: 0.9em; font-weight: normal;">    

  In this note, we integrate game-theoretic modeling with LLM-based multi-agent simulation to study belief-dependent crisis bargaining. Using Russia-Ukraine negotiations as a test case, we model a sequential game where Ukraine, Russia, and the US make strategic choices under uncertainty about adversary trustworthiness. Drawing on foreign policy typologies (Gravelle et al., 2017, 2020), we assign each leader one of four predispositions—Cooperative Internationalism (CI), Isolationism (ISO), Militant Internationalism (MI), and Global Justice (GJ)—while manipulating trust perceptions of Putin. This 2 × 4³ design yields 128 scenarios via AutoGen. Our results partiallyconfirm predictions while revealing asymmetries across actors: all actors conformstrongly to theory under hawkish orientations (ISO/MI), but under dovish orientations (CI/GJ), only Zelensky shows substantial cooperation (negotiating and implementing 75% and 50% of the time, respectively), while Putin never accepts demands and Trump never grants sanctions relief. Our LLM-based multi-agent simulation may help bridge the gap between game-theoretic models and behavioral implementation while providing a computational sandbox for exploring belief-dependent bargaining theories.
    </div>    
  </details>


- Electoral Reform and Issue Attention in Legislative Oversight: From SNTV to Mixed-Member Majoritarian System in Taiwan. (with <u><a href="https://sites.google.com/view/calvin-yuceng-liao/home" style="color: #9ab8b8;">Yu-Ceng Liao</a></u> and <u><a href="https://yitingw.com" style="color: #9ab8b8;">Yi-ting Wang</a></u>) _Under Review_ {{< badge href="https://www.dropbox.com/scl/fi/n5wjjs063m3114c608ank/Electoral_Reform_and_Issue_Attention_in_Legislative_Oversight.pdf?rlkey=cne737myrmzxsqb3s0ojgjr6s&dl=0" icon="fa-file-pdf" text="PDF" >}}

  <details class="abstract" style="font-weight: bold;">
    <summary>Abstract <br> (Conference: 2023 EPSA ⬥ 2025 TPSA)</summary>
    <div style="font-size: 0.9em; font-weight: normal;">    
    This research note examines how Taiwan's electoral reform—from Single Non-Transferable Vote (SNTV) in multi-member district (MMD) to a single-member district (SMD) -- dominant mixed-member majoritarian system (MMM) -- affects how closely legislators align with their party's policy attention. While existing work explains which issues legislators emphasize and where they position themselves, less is known about how electoral systems shape the cohesion of issue attention within parties. We address this gap using 1999–2019 interpellations from Taiwanese legislators annotated with 422 fixed topic keywords. We introduce a new application of Wordfish: instead of estimating ideology, we scale legislators' issue attention and compute their divergence from the party. We find significantly higher intra-party convergence under SMD than SNTV. Local socioeconomic conditions strongly influence attention variation under SNTV but are substantially attenuated after reform. The findings highlight a key institutional trade-off: SNTV incentivizes geographically differentiated agendas, whereas SMD promotes party-aligned priorities.
    </div>
