PhD Research Project: Essays in Taiwan Legislative Politics

Mayors and Pork Barrel: Legislative Experience Bias in Intergovernmental Transfers to Taiwan Municipalities (Presented in 2019 EPSA; Accepted in 2020 Asian Polmeth Poster Session at HKU, cancellation due to unforeseen circumstances in Hong Kong; 2021 Annual Conference of the American Association for Chinese Studies) [Github ][Slides ][Paper ]

This paper examines the effect, if any, behind the municipalities whose mayors have more prior experience as members of parliament (MPs) are allocated disproportionately more fiscal supports. Mayors with experience as MPs have strong electoral bases, which is used to stay on good terms with businesses and cultivate clientele. Further, it helps to develop skills to broker compromises with opposition parties, interact well with constituencies, and manage relationships with the press, which will, in turn, increase MPs incentive to allocate more on the local strongholds. Using unbalanced panel dataset regarding the allocation of Revenue Support Grant among local governments, we identified the effect of a mayors prior political experience in the legislature on pork distribution in Taiwan. We found that a mayor with more years of experience as a MP is more likely to be allocated more fiscal expenditures to the municipality, particularly those MPs who had membership in a standing committee. By contrast, the mayor’s experience in ministries is not statistically significant in explaining the change in the grant received by municipalities. The findings suggest that compared to experience as a central government official, a legislative career is a leading cause for fiscal supports that local government receives.

Keywords: distributive politics, pork barrel, intergovernmental grants

Electoral Reform and Disunited Polarization: Evidence from Legislative Roll Calls (Presented in the Virtual 2020 APSA with Li Tang, Birmingham) [GitHub ][Slides ][Paper ]

This article investigates the strategic (inter- and intra-) party positioning in re- sponse to an electoral system transition from single non-transferable vote (SNTV) to single-member districts (SMDs) by studying the electoral reform in Taiwan and pro- vides empirical evidence to the following questions. Does the switch of electoral system from SNTV to SMDs mitigate political polarization among parties? Does it influence the intra-party unitedness (cohesiveness), and thus, change legislators’ ideological po- sitioning? To this end, we estimate individual legislator’s ideological positions from sessional roll call votes continuously covering ex-ante and ex-post periods of the reform. Expectation Maximization (EM) algorithm is applied to a dynamic ideal point model to estimate each legislator’s position from 1992 to 2015, where individual recursively updates her prior of ideal point. The preliminary finding suggests that in the electoral transition from SNTV to SMDs, ideological positions between two major parties are drastically polarized. In the meanwhile, this electoral transition significantly dis-unifies co-partisan legislators on the ideological spectrum. In addition, co-partisan legislators became ideologically distant (disunited) while opponents polarized themselves. These findings help clarify possible outcomes of the electoral reform from SNTV to SMDs, shedding light on party polarization and party competition in modern democracies.

Keywords: electoral reform, single-member districts (SMDs), roll call, political polarization



Side Projects

Factionalism and the Red Guards under Mao’s China: Ideal Point Estimation Using Text Data with Yi-Nung Tsai (National Chengchi University), Dechun Zhang (Leiden University), presented in 2021 AsianPolmeth Presentation VIII [GitHub ][Slides ][Paper ]

This article estimates the spatial positioning of the political elites and participants in the Cultural Revolution through analyzing expressed political views in propaganda publication in China. Prior theoretical research indicates that the political elites and the Red Guards lost control of the social movement, and it evolved into sheer verbal violence and physical skirmishes across all provinces in the end. We show that in this political chaos, the Red Guards and the elite were dynamically fractioned, as is reflected in self-printed propaganda, such as big-character posters (Dazibao 大字报) and tabloids (Xiaobao 小报). This paper also develops a novel approach that augments TextRank algorithm to extract keywords and Chinese phrases, on top of the Poisson scaling model (Wordfish) to estimate the differences of spatial positions using the extracted textual key terms. Results are shown to be consistent with the literature on the Cultural Revolution.

Keywords：Text as Data, Cultural Revolution, Mass movements, the Red Guards

Gendered and Ethnic Differences in Legislative Responsiveness: Evidence from a Field Experiment with Taiwanese Councilors with Shih-chan Dai (University of Massachusetts-Amherst)

One of the underlying principles of democracy is the idea of equality. Equality is manifested in the spirit that people of different demographic groups receive non-discriminatory treatment by the government. In democratic regimes, explicit de jure discrimination targeting minority groups is literally nonexistent. However, we may still observe de facto and implicit discrimination against minorities because of the lasting effect of social prejudice. This paper explores these subtle forms of implicit discrimination by adopting two competing hypotheses (full equality versus social dominance). It examines if legislators are less responsive to constituents of minority groups by conducting a field experiment. We send to Taiwanese city councilors emails with the same content but different names which indicate the gender and ethnicity of a constituent. Legislative responsiveness is operationalized in three different ways and we examine if the gender and ethnicity of a constituent affect legislative responsiveness.

Keywords: responsiveness, minority groups, field experiment, constituency service, discrimination

Income Inequality, Party Misperception and Democracy Distrust with Royce Carroll (University of Essex), Li Tang (University of Birmingham), Tzu-Ping Liu (University of California at Davis), accepted in 2021 EPSA Virtual Conference.

This paper studies an important political channel, through which income inequality transforms into dissatisfaction with democracy and distrust in government, using multiple waves of British Election Study from 2015 to 2019. We first document that households of different wealth have persistent gaps in the degree of satisfaction with democracy and in governmental trust, regardless of their gender, education, party affiliation, survey waves, etc. Low-income earners are more dissatisfied with how democracy works and more distrustful of the government than affluent households. Then we show that income inequality has causal effect on misperception about political parties, that is, the the extend to which households wrongly placed their beliefs of parties’ left-right position from reality, measured by Chapel Hill Expert Surveys. Households of low income perceive party positions less accurately than households of affluence and therefore, have larger misperception. Finally, we demonstrate the causality from the size of party misperception to dissatisfaction with democracy and distrust in government: observing the the actual party positions, low-income households are more disappointed by the bigger discrepancy between their perceived and actual party placement, and become more dissatisfied and distrustful of the democracy and the government.

Keywords: income inequality, Chapel Hill Expert Surveys, party misperception, political trust
